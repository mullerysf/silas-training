Program Rules

Progressive Overload

When you hit the TOP of the rep range on ALL sets, add 5 lbs next session. Log every weight so you can track this.

Rest Between Sets

Big lifts (squat, bench, deadlift): 2–3 min. Accessory work: 60–90 sec. Supersets: move directly to the next exercise, then rest 90 sec.

Nutrition Basics

Hit ~0.7–1g protein per pound of bodyweight daily. Eat a meal or shake within 30 min after lifting. Don't train fasted — eat something beforehand.

90/90 Hip Flexor Stretch
Sit with front leg at 90° and rear leg at 90° to the side. Tall spine, lean gently forward over front shin. Don't force it — breathe into the stretch.
How to do it:
  1. Sit on the floor. Front leg bent at 90° in front of you, shin parallel to your body.

  3. Sit tall — don't round your back. Gently lean forward over your front shin.

  5. Hold the full 2 minutes. It will deepen as you relax into it.

Kneeling Hip Flexor Stretch
Low lunge, rear knee on ground. Squeeze the glute of the rear leg — this is key. Shift forward until you feel the front of the hip. Hold still, don't rock.

  3. Shift your hips forward until you feel a stretch at the front of the rear hip.
  4. Stay tall — don't lean your upper body forward.
  5. Hold still and breathe. The stretch is at the front of the hip, not the knee.

Tennis
Thoracic Rotation (T-Spine)
Sit cross-legged or kneel. Place hand behind head, rotate elbow toward the ceiling as far as possible. T-spine rotation = serve power and forehand snap. Critical for tennis.
How to do it:
  1. Sit cross-legged on the floor, or kneel.
  2. Place one hand behind your head, elbow pointing out.
  3. Rotate that elbow up toward the ceiling as far as you can.
  4. Eyes follow the elbow. Hold 1-2 seconds at the top.
  5. Return slowly. The rotation should come from your upper back, not your lower back.
10 reps/side

Tennis
Shoulder Sleeper Stretch
Lie on your side (dominant arm down). Arm at 90°, use other hand to gently press wrist toward the floor. Protects the rotator cuff from overuse injury — especially important for heavy serving.
How to do it:
  1. Lie on your side on the dominant arm (right side if right-handed).
  2. Your arm should be at 90° — upper arm pointing away from body, forearm pointing toward ceiling.
  3. With your other hand, gently press your wrist down toward the floor.
  4. You'll feel a stretch in the back of your shoulder.
  5. Don't force it. Hold and breathe. This protects your rotator cuff from serving stress.
2 min/side
✓
Tennis
Ankle Circles + Calf Stretch
10 slow ankle circles each direction, then calf stretch against a wall — straight leg and bent knee. Ankles take a beating with all the lateral movement in tennis.
How to do it:
  1. For circles: sit or stand, lift one foot off the floor. Draw 10 big slow circles each direction.
  2. For calf stretch (straight leg): stand facing a wall, hands on it. Step one foot back, press heel into floor.
  3. For calf stretch (bent knee): same position but bend the back knee slightly. Targets the deeper calf muscle.
  4. Hold each stretch 45-60 seconds per side.
  5. Don't bounce — hold still and breathe.
1 min/side

Pigeon Pose
From downward dog, bring one shin forward parallel to your hands and lower hips toward the floor. Deep hip opener — targets glute med and external rotators. Breathe and relax into it, don't push.
How to do it:
  1. From a push-up or downward dog position, bring your right shin forward and lay it on the floor.

World's Greatest Stretch
From lunge, place same-side hand inside foot on the floor. Rotate other arm to the sky, eyes following. Then drop elbow to the floor inside foot. Hits hips, thoracic spine, hamstrings, and shoulders all in one movement.
How to do it:
  1. Start in a lunge position — right foot forward, left knee on floor.

  4. Then drop that elbow to the floor inside your right foot.
  5. 5 slow reps per side. This hits hip flexors, thoracic spine, hamstrings, and shoulders all at once.

Cat-Cow
On hands and knees. Inhale — drop belly, lift head and tailbone (cow). Exhale — round spine to ceiling, tuck chin and pelvis (cat). Slow and deliberate. Keeps the spine loose and healthy.
How to do it:
  1. Start on hands and knees, wrists under shoulders, knees under hips.
  2. COW (inhale): let your belly drop toward the floor, lift your head and tailbone up.
  3. CAT (exhale): round your spine up toward the ceiling, tuck your chin and pelvis under.
  4. Move slowly between the two. Let your breath guide the movement.
  5. 10 slow, full reps. This keeps the spine healthy and mobile.

Tips for Success

Sleep Is the Gains

At 14, you're getting a growth hormone surge during sleep. 8–9 hours isn't optional — it's when you actually build muscle from your training. Prioritize this above everything.

Tennis + Lifting = Smart Scheduling

You're lifting Tue/Thu/Sat so Mon, Wed, and Fri are tennis days. Monday and Wednesday are group training, Friday is serve practice at the courts once cleared. If tennis matches return on Saturdays, the third lift day can move back to Friday.

Tracking = Progress

Log every weight you use. If you hit the top rep range on all sets, bump the weight 5 lbs next session. That's how strength is built — systematically, not randomly.

Nutrition

Protein Is the Priority

Aim for 0.7–1g of protein per pound of bodyweight daily — so around 100–140g if you're 140 lbs. Best sources: chicken, eggs, Greek yogurt, cottage cheese, ground beef, tuna, and milk. A protein shake after lifting is an easy way to hit your numbers without overthinking it.

The Post-Workout Window

Eat within 30–45 minutes of finishing a lift. Your muscles are primed to absorb nutrients right after training. A shake + banana, or chocolate milk + some chicken, works great. Don't wait until you're starving — that window matters.

Carbs Are Not the Enemy

At your age and activity level, carbs are fuel — for lifting, for tennis, and for recovery. Rice, oats, potatoes, fruit, and bread are all fine. Eat more carbs on training days, especially before and after workouts. Don't try to cut carbs while also trying to build muscle — it doesn't work.

Eat Before You Train

Never lift or play tennis on an empty stomach. A meal 1.5–2 hours before, or a light snack 30–45 min before, gives you the energy to actually push hard. Banana + peanut butter, a bowl of oatmeal, or toast with eggs all work well.

Hydration on Tennis Days

You're training outside in Florida summer heat — dehydration will tank your performance fast. Drink water consistently all day, not just during training. On tennis days, aim for at least 16–20 oz in the hour before you start, and sip every changeover. Add electrolytes (Liquid IV, Gatorade, etc.) if you're sweating heavily.

Calories: Eat Enough

Building muscle requires a caloric surplus — you need to eat slightly more than you burn. At 14 with this activity level, that means eating big, consistent meals. If you're not gaining any weight over several weeks, you're not eating enough. Don't be afraid of food — this is the one time in your life where eating more is part of the program.
